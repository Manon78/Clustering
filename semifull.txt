Hi Marianna,
 
I understand your concern about the superficial aspect of the houses, and thank you for your question. I'll be happy to explain why and how this topic is linked to very profound sociological trends and my observations thereof.
 
I believe that the Russians I have met have an incredible capacity to make an abrupt break with the past, and this is confirmed by centuries of Russian history (reversal upon quick reveral of political situations). Whereas in England and in France we are both slowed and aided by our strong attachment to the past, Russians are capable of extremely swiftly changing their entire mind-set.
 
A striking example taken from literary history comes from the Moscow-based Futurist movement Гилея, who wrote in the manifesto "A Slap in the Face of Public Taste" that Pushkin, Dostoevsky and the like should be "heaved overboard from the steamship of modernity". This is not a lone example, but can be found in different form in matters of personal life (swift career changes observed nowadays in Russia), history (Peter the Great's extremely rapid restructuring of society, the 1917 revolution, the 1990's) and architecture. The trend that this uncovers is the Russian understanding that it is faster and more efficient to destroy and rebuild than to renovate what already exists.In this stands my main connection between the cultural observations I have made and the solid, enticing example of wooden houses. Almost invariably, Russians I have spoken to claimed that it would be smarter to destroy and build afresh the old wooden houses of Siberia than to have them renovated for their historical significance. This, I believe, strongly constrasts my own and the English cultures, where ancient objects gain value if only because of being ancient.
 
There are several other sociological observations that can be made when examining these houses, such as but not limited to the evolution of land-ownership from before the Soviet Union, through those 60 years and then again during the post-Soviet Union period. There are plenty of observations to be made regarding urban pressure, the winners and the victims of a capitalist city-plan. One can discuss how family relationships have changed or remained stable, focussing upon who occupies the family house (many of the houses I have researched are maintained in one family line). These are only a few of my sociological observations, which I will best put into form though a focus on the small wooden houses of Siberia.
